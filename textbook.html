Photosynthesis

Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants manufacture their own food. This process takes place in the leaves of plants where chlorophyll is present. Chlorophyll absorbs light energy from the sun which is used to combine carbon dioxide from the air and water from the soil to form glucose. Oxygen is released as a by-product during this process. Photosynthesis is important because it provides food for plants and forms the base of all food chains. Without photosynthesis, there would be no life on Earth as plants supply oxygen needed by living organisms for respiration.

To break it down even further: inside the tiny green cells of a leaf, a molecule called chlorophyll captures sunlight. That energy powers a chemical reaction that takes carbon dioxide (what we breathe out) and water (from the ground) and turns them into sugar. The sugar gives the plant energy to grow, while oxygen – the stuff we breathe – drifts out into the air. In scientific terms the simple equation is: 6 CO₂ + 6 H₂O + light → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6 O₂.

Because every animal either eats plants or eats something that ate plants, the whole food web depends on this green food-making trick. It's also why forests and grasses are sometimes called the "lungs of the planet" – they keep our air full of oxygen. So when you study or draw diagrams, remember: a leaf isn't just a leaf, it's a tiny solar-powered factory!
